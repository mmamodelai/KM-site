San Diego Housing Commission v. Neil & Kidder Mathews
San Diego Superior Court · Filed August 3, 2021 · Settled August 2022
Settled — $1,000,000

What happened: Kidder Mathews broker Jim Neil was hired by the San Diego Housing Commission to advise on hotel acquisitions for homeless housing — two deals totaling over $67 million. While advising the city, Neil purchased 40,000 shares of Chatham Lodging Trust — the counterparty selling the Mission Valley hotel — before the deal closed. He did not disclose this conflict.

The overcharges: Neil also charged commissions far exceeding his contractual cap of $250,000: $592,500 on the Kearny Mesa deal and $502,500 on the Mission Valley deal — more than double the cap on each transaction.

The outcome: Kidder Mathews and Neil paid $1,000,000 to settle ($845,000 to the Housing Commission, $155,000 to the City). Neil was permanently disbarred from all San Diego city business. Kidder Mathews accepted a 2-year ban from city contracts.

Non-Arms-Length Relationship

Standard Property Management Practice
  • Conduct competitive bidding (minimum 3 bids)
  • Disclose any pre-existing relationships with bidders
  • Require conditional lien releases before each progress payment
  • Require unconditional releases from prior payment before next payment
  • Verify subcontractors are being paid
  • Use joint check arrangements when red flags arise
  • Know your GC's financial condition and litigation history
What Kidder Mathews Did — Via Del Campo
  • Apparent award to RSI without documented competitive bidding
  • Did not disclose existing BOMA professional relationship with RSI leadership
  • Did not require a single lien release from RSI during the project
  • Did not verify subcontractors were paid before releasing funds to RSI
  • RSI collected payments; subcontractor went unpaid $142,275
  • $142,275 mechanic's lien now encumbers client's property
  • RSI has pending labor class action — publicly available since March 2023
California Civil Code § 8202 — Property Owner Warning (Preliminary Notice)
"You can protect yourself from paying twice by requiring your contractor to provide a signed release by the person or firm that has given you this notice before making payment to your contractor."
This statutory language appears on every California Preliminary Notice — including the Preliminary Notice filed by S&W Roof Tear Off on February 4, 2026. As the professional property manager, Kidder Mathews received this notice and was legally informed of S&W's lien rights. It did not require lien releases from RSI.
